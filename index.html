WHAT ARE MUCOPOLYSACCHARIDOSES & LYSOSOMAL STORAGE DISEASES?

Lysosomal storage disorders are a group of inherited metabolic diseases caused by defects in lysosomal function, most often due to deficiencies in enzymes responsible for degrading macromolecules. These defects lead to the accumulation of undegraded substrates within lysosomes, disrupting cellular homeostasis and ultimately impairing tissue and organ function.

The mucopolysaccharidoses (MPS) are a defined subset of LSDs resulting from impaired degradation of glycosaminoglycans (GAGs), key components of the extracellular matrix. Mutations affecting specific lysosomal enzymes prevent the stepwise breakdown of GAGs, leading to their progressive accumulation across multiple organ systems.

Although substrate storage is the primary defect, disease progression is driven by broader cellular dysfunction, impaired autophagy, and inflammatory responses. Clinically, both LSDs and MPS present as multisystem disorders, often affecting the central nervous system, skeletal system, and visceral organs, with severity influenced by residual enzyme activity.

RESEARCH

Our research program focuses on advancing therapeutic strategies for mucopolysaccharidoses and lysosomal storage disorders through comprehensive natural history studies, molecular characterization, and translational research aimed at developing and optimizing patient-centered treatments.

ENZYME REPLACEMENT THERAPY

Development and optimization of enzyme replacement therapies for MPS disorders, including novel delivery systems and formulations to improve therapeutic efficacy and patient outcomes.

GENE THERAPY

Investigation of gene therapy approaches for lysosomal storage disorders, focusing on safe and effective delivery methods to provide long-term therapeutic benefits.

NATURAL HISTORY STUDIES

Comprehensive characterization of disease progression, phenotype-genotype correlations, and identification of prognostic biomarkers to inform therapeutic development and clinical trial design.

BIOMARKER DISCOVERY

Identification and validation of biomarkers for early diagnosis, disease monitoring, and treatment response assessment in mucopolysaccharidoses and related disorders.

NEWBORN SCREENING

Development and implementation of newborn screening strategies for early detection of MPS disorders, enabling timely intervention and improved patient outcomes.

ANIMAL MODELS

Creation and characterization of animal models for MPS disorders to study disease mechanisms and evaluate novel therapeutic approaches in preclinical settings.

ADRIANA MONTAÑO PH.D.

Vice Dean for Research | Professor of Pediatrics, and Biochemistry and Molecular Biology

Dr. Montaño is an internationally recognized scientist with over 30 years of research experience on lysosomal storage disorders, particularly Mucopolysaccharidoses (MPS). She has published over 100 scientific papers, participated in more than 150 conferences globally, and holds 12 patents. READ MORE

Dr. Montaño is an internationally recognized scientist who has made truly outstanding contributions to the field of Mucopolysaccharidoses (MPS) research.

Dr. Montaño has over 30 years of research experience on lysosomal storage disorders. Her research has set the basis for the understanding of Morquio A disease (MPS IVA) and Sly disease (MPS VII) by providing insights into the natural history of patients showing their heterogeneity in phenotype and genotype. She has developed mouse models for Morquio A disease instrumental to carry out pre-clinical trials to assess novel treatments including enzyme replacement therapy, gene therapy, and substrate reduction therapy. Her work also includes discovery of biomarkers and the development of newborn screening for patients with MPS, which is fundamental for early detection and treatment of patients.

Dr. Montaño received her B.Sc. degree from the University of Los Andes (Bogotá, Colombia), and Ph.D. degree from Gifu University (Japan), where she was a Monbusho Scholar. She received a Postdoctoral Fellowship from the Japan Society for the Promotion of Science (JSPS) to study molecular evolution at the Graduate University for Advanced Studies (Japan). She also served as a Postdoctoral Fellow at Kobe Pharmaceutical University (Japan) where she studied chondroitin sulfate metabolism. Dr. Montaño received a M.Sc. degree from Saint Louis University in Health Outcomes Research and Evaluation Sciences.

Dr. Montaño is a recipient of multiple extramural grants and awards and has published over 100 scientific papers. She has participated in more than 150 conferences around the globe and holds 12 patents.
